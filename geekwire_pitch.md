Three proposed campuses in the Tri-Cities — Amazon's $4.8B Wallula Gap project, Atlas Agro's Richland DC1, and Trammell Crow's Lewis & Clark Ranch — all score at the floor of a water availability index I built across Washington State. The floor is not zero by construction; it reflects 30-year precipitation normals. The eastern Columbia Basin is that dry.

I built a five-indicator geospatial siting model for Washington: transmission proximity, water availability, seismic hazard, flood risk, and community burden (replicating the EPA EJScreen Demographic Index). Scored across a 14 km grid of 974 cells covering the state, with adjustable weights. The Quincy cluster — the dominant hub — scores 0.988 on grid access and 0.189 on water. A site 50-80 km west achieves a 30% better composite score with equivalent transmission access. The industry is moving in the opposite direction, deeper into arid eastern Washington, at the moment the state is legislating mandatory water-use reporting.

The full analysis is live and interactive at datacenters.simonhansedasi.com. Weights are adjustable via slider; the map, recommended zones, and site rankings update in real time.
